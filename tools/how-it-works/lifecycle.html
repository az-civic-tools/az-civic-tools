Life of a Bill

From napkin sketch to the law of the land (or the recycling bin)

Every law in Arizona started as an idea. Most ideas die. Here's the obstacle course a bill has to survive — and the many, many ways it can fail.

Step 1: The Idea

Every bill starts as an idea. Maybe a constituent complained to their lawmaker about potholes. Maybe a lobbyist pitched a tax break over lunch. Maybe a lawmaker read a news story and said "someone should do something." In Arizona, anyone can suggest a bill idea — but only a lawmaker can officially introduce one.

Fun fact: Lobbyists draft many of the bills themselves. Lawmakers are busy — if someone hands them a ready-to-go bill, that's one less thing to do.

Step 2: Finding a Sponsor

A bill needs a sponsor — a lawmaker willing to put their name on it and shepherd it through the process. The sponsor is listed as the "prime sponsor" and others can sign on as co-sponsors. Bills can have sponsors from both parties, though that's increasingly rare.

Bills in the House start with "HB" (House Bill). Senate bills start with "SB." Resolutions use "HCR" or "SCR" for concurrent resolutions.

Step 3: Introduction & First Read

The bill is officially filed and read into the record — that's the "First Read." In the House, the Speaker assigns it to one or more committees right away. In the Senate, committee assignments happen on Second Read. This is a critical moment: which committee a bill goes to can determine its fate, because the committee chair controls whether it ever gets a hearing.

Dead on Arrival

If the committee chair doesn't like your bill, they can simply never schedule it for a hearing. No hearing = no vote = dead bill. This is the #1 way bills die in Arizona.

Step 4: Committee Hearing

This is where the real work happens. The committee holds a public hearing where lawmakers discuss the bill, hear testimony from supporters and opponents, and decide what to do. The public can participate through the Request to Speak (RTS) system — registering for or against a bill from their phone. After discussion, the committee votes: Do Pass (DP), Do Pass Amended (DPA), Held (H), or Withdrawn (W/D).

Real Example

HCR 2053 (2025) — a bill to remove legislative immunity for traffic violations — passed the House 37-20 but was assigned to Senate committees that never scheduled a hearing. The committee chair simply refused to put it on the agenda. Dead.

Your Voice Matters Here

RTS lets you register support or opposition on any bill being heard in committee. Lawmakers see these counts. Use it at azleg.gov.

Held or Failed in Committee

The committee chair can hold a bill indefinitely, or the committee can vote it down. Either way, the bill is dead unless a striker gives it new life in another vehicle.

Step 5: Rules Committee

Every bill that passes its assigned committee must also pass the Rules Committee. In the House, this is called "Proper for Consideration" (PFC). In the Senate, it's "Constitution and Proper Form" (C&P). The Rules Committee checks that the bill doesn't violate the Arizona or U.S. Constitution and that it's properly formatted. This is usually a formality, but occasionally bills get flagged here.

Step 6: Committee of the Whole (COW)

Now the entire chamber — all 60 House members or all 30 Senators — meets as one big committee. This is where floor amendments happen. Lawmakers can propose changes, debate the bill, and try to modify it before the final vote. This is also where "strike everything" amendments can completely replace a bill's contents with entirely new text.

A bill can be "retained on calendar" during COW — meaning lawmakers skip voting on it and put it back on the schedule for later. This is the legislative equivalent of hitting the snooze button.

Step 7: Third Read & Floor Vote

The moment of truth. The bill gets its Third Read and the full chamber votes. In the House, you need 31 out of 60 votes. In the Senate, 16 out of 30. Every vote is recorded and public — you can see exactly how each lawmaker voted on azleg.gov. If the bill passes, it moves to the other chamber to start the process all over again.

Real Example

SB1745 (2026) — a bill to limit local excise taxes — went to a Senate floor vote and failed 15-15. A tie means failure. The majority can sometimes retain the bill and try again later, but in this case, the bill never got another vote.

Failed on the Floor

If the bill doesn't get enough votes, it's dead. Sometimes the majority party can retain it and bring it back for another vote days or weeks later — but if that doesn't happen, it's over.

Step 8: Crossing Over

If the bill passes the first chamber, it crosses over to the other one and starts the process from the beginning — committee assignment, hearing, COW, Third Read. The bill has to survive both chambers to reach the governor. This is also where deadlines matter: bills that don't clear the crossover chamber's committees by the crossover deadline are dead.

Real Example

HB2145 (2026) — a bill about fuel formulation waivers — passed the House 31-22 but was assigned to a Senate committee that voted "Not Recommended." The bill failed in the crossover chamber's committee and died.

Stalled in Crossover

Passed one chamber but couldn't get through the other side's committees before the deadline. Very common — hundreds of bills die this way every session.

Step 9: Conference Committee

What happens when the House and Senate pass different versions of the same bill? They form a Conference Committee — a small group of lawmakers from each chamber who negotiate a compromise. If they agree on a final version, both chambers vote on the compromise. If they can't agree, the bill dies.

Conference committees have their own deadline. In 2026, it's April 17th. If they can't work it out by then, the bill is dead.

Step 10: The Governor's Desk

The bill has survived both chambers and lands on the governor's desk. The governor has three choices: Sign it (it becomes law), Veto it (it's dead unless the legislature overrides with a 2/3 vote in each chamber), or Do nothing (if the legislature is still in session, the bill becomes law after 5 days without a signature — the "pocket signature"). If the legislature has adjourned, the governor gets 10 days.

Real Example: Signed

SB1543 (2025) — the Axon headquarters bill — was signed by Governor Hobbs after passing both chambers.

Real Example: Vetoed

SB1164 (2025) — the "Arizona ICE Act" — was vetoed by Governor Hobbs. In 2025, Hobbs set a modern record by vetoing over 170 bills.

Vetoed

The governor said no. Override requires 2/3 in each chamber (40 House, 20 Senate), which almost never happens in Arizona.

The Many Ways a Bill Can Die

The legislative process is an obstacle course, and most bills don't survive it. Here's every place a bill can meet its end:

  • Never scheduled for a hearing — the most common cause of death, by far
  • Failed or held in committee — voted down or simply shelved forever
  • Failed on the floor — couldn't get enough votes in the full chamber
  • Stalled in the crossover chamber — passed one side, died on the other
  • Conference committee deadlock — the two chambers couldn't agree on a final version
  • Vetoed by the governor — made it all the way, only to get shot down at the finish line
  • Missed a deadline — the calendar ran out before the process finished
Of the roughly 1,800 bills introduced in a typical Arizona session, fewer than 400 make it to the governor's desk, and many of those get vetoed.

Special Paths

Not every bill follows the standard route. A few special types play by different rules.

Concurrent Resolutions (SCR/HCR)

Concurrent resolutions skip the governor entirely. They go straight to the ballot for voters to decide (for constitutional amendments) or to the Secretary of State (for memorials and other non-binding measures). The governor has no say — these are purely a legislative action.

Strikers

Dead bills can be resurrected. A "strike everything" amendment replaces a living bill's entire text with completely new language. The original bill becomes a vehicle — its number lives on, but its contents are gone. This is how controversial measures sometimes sneak through late in session.

Emergency Clauses

Normally, new laws don't take effect until 90 days after the session ends. But bills with an emergency clause take effect immediately upon the governor's signature. The catch: emergency clauses require a 2/3 vote in each chamber instead of a simple majority.
