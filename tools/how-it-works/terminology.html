The Dictionary

Every confusing word the legislature uses, translated into English.

The Arizona Legislature loves its jargon. COW, striker, sine die ... it sounds like a different language. That's by design. Here's your cheat sheet.

Process

1st Read

Process

When new bills are introduced and read into the record for the first time. In the House, bills get assigned to a committee right away. In the Senate, committee assignments wait until 2nd Read.

Related: 2nd Read, Introduced
Source: azleg.gov

2nd Read

Process

The second time a bill is read on the floor. In the Senate, this is when committee assignments happen. Think of it as the bill officially entering the pipeline.

Related: 1st Read, 3rd Read, Assigned
Source: azleg.gov

3rd Read

Process

The final reading ... this is the big vote. The entire chamber votes on the bill. If it passes, it moves to the other chamber (or to the governor if it already passed both).

Related: Committee of the Whole, Passed
Source: azleg.gov

Assigned

Process

A bill has been sent to a committee. This is where the committee chair decides if your bill lives or dies ... they can simply never schedule a hearing and the bill just disappears.

Related: Standing Committee, Hearing, Held
Source: azleg.gov

Crossover Week

Process

The week when bills that passed one chamber on 3rd Read get submitted to the other chamber. It's like halftime in the legislative session. If your bill hasn't crossed over, its chances are slim.

Related: 3rd Read, Transmit to House/Senate
Source: azleg.gov

Do Pass (DP)

Process

The committee voted to approve the bill as-is. Green light. The bill moves forward to the next step without any changes.

Related: Do Pass Amended, Hearing
Source: azleg.gov

Do Pass Amended (DPA)

Process

The committee approved the bill, but they changed some things first. The bill moves forward with those amendments baked in.

Related: Do Pass, Do Pass Amended/SE
Source: azleg.gov

Do Pass Amended/SE (Strike Everything)

Process

The committee approved a completely rewritten version of the bill. The original text is gone ... replaced with something entirely new. This is the famous "striker." A bill about parks could become a bill about taxes. Welcome to Arizona.

Related: Striker, Do Pass Amended
Source: azleg.gov

Failed

Process

The bill didn't get enough votes to pass, either in committee or on the floor. Game over (usually). A motion to reconsider can sometimes bring it back, but don't hold your breath.

Related: Motion to Reconsider, Passed
Source: azleg.gov

Final Read

Process

When the opposite chamber amends a bill, it comes back to the original chamber for one more vote on the amended version. Think of it as the last chance to approve or reject the changes the other side made.

Related: Returned to Original Chamber, Passed Amended
Source: azleg.gov

Hearing

Process

A committee meeting where lawmakers discuss and vote on bills. Open to the public ... you can show up and testify. This is one of the best ways to make your voice heard.

Related: Request to Speak, Agenda, Standing Committee
Source: azleg.gov

Held

Process

A bill on a committee agenda that the chair decided not to move forward. It just ... sits there. Not dead on paper, but not going anywhere either. Legislative limbo.

Related: Assigned, Hearing
Source: azleg.gov

Introduced

Process

A bill has been officially filed and read into the record. Also called "dropped in the hopper." This is day one of a bill's life. It exists now.

Related: 1st Read, Prefiled, Sponsor
Source: azleg.gov

Motion to Reconsider

Process

A do-over request. A lawmaker asks to re-examine a previous vote or committee action. It's a procedural tool to bring something back from the dead, but it requires enough votes to succeed.

Related: Failed, 3rd Read
Source: azleg.gov

Passed

Process

The bill got enough votes. In the House, that's 31 out of 60. In the Senate, 16 out of 30. Simple majority wins. The bill moves on to the next stage.

Related: 3rd Read, Failed, Transmit to Governor
Source: azleg.gov, Arizona Constitution

Passed Amended

Process

The bill passed, but with changes made during debate. It's still moving forward, but the version that passed isn't the same as the version that was introduced.

Related: Passed, Final Read
Source: azleg.gov

Prefiled

Process

Bills introduced before the session officially starts. Lawmakers are planners sometimes. Prefiling lets them hit the ground running on day one.

Related: Introduced, Regular Session
Source: azleg.gov

Retained on Calendar

Process

A motion to skip voting on a bill during COW and put it back on the calendar for later. Basically, "we're not ready to vote on this yet." It keeps the bill alive but kicks the can down the road.

Related: Committee of the Whole, Calendar
Source: azleg.gov

Returned to Original Chamber

Process

A bill passed both chambers but was amended by the second chamber. Now the first chamber has to approve those changes. If they don't like the amendments, it might go to a conference committee.

Related: Final Read, Conference Committee
Source: azleg.gov

Signed

Process

The governor signed the bill. It's law now. Most laws take effect 90 days after the session ends, unless the bill has an emergency clause (which requires a 2/3 vote).

Related: Transmit to Governor, Vetoed
Source: Arizona Constitution, Article V, Section 7

Sine Die

Process

Latin for "without a day." When the legislature officially closes up shop for the session. Everyone goes home. Any bills that didn't pass are dead ... they'd need to be reintroduced next session to try again.

Related: Regular Session
Source: azleg.gov

Striker (Strike Everything Amendment)

Process

An amendment that deletes every word of the original bill and replaces it with completely new text. Often used to revive dead bills by hijacking a living bill's "vehicle." The Trojan Horse of Arizona politics. A bill about road signs could suddenly become a bill about election law. Pay attention to these.

Related: Do Pass Amended/SE
Source: azleg.gov

Substitute Bill

Process

A bill that replaces another bill with the same language in the other chamber. If the Senate passes SB1234 and the House has HB5678 with identical text, one becomes a substitute for the other. It's a procedural shortcut to avoid duplicating work.

Related: Crossover Week
Source: azleg.gov

Transmit to Governor

Process

The bill passed both chambers and is on its way to the governor's desk. The finish line is in sight. The governor has 5 days to sign it, veto it, or let it become law without a signature.

Related: Signed, Vetoed
Source: Arizona Constitution, Article V, Section 7

Transmit to House/Senate

Process

The bill passed one chamber and is being sent to the other chamber. Halfway there. The bill now starts the committee process all over again in the second chamber.

Related: Crossover Week, 3rd Read
Source: azleg.gov

Transmit to Secretary of State

Process

For concurrent resolutions (ballot referrals) ... they skip the governor entirely and go straight to the Secretary of State to be placed on the ballot. The voters get the final say, not the governor.

Related: Concurrent Resolution, Referendum
Source: Arizona Constitution

Vetoed

Process

The governor said no. The bill is dead unless the legislature overrides with a 2/3 vote in each chamber (which almost never happens in Arizona). Governor Hobbs vetoed over 170 bills in the 2025 session alone.

Related: Signed, Transmit to Governor
Source: Arizona Constitution, Article V, Section 7

W/D (Withdrawn)

Process

The sponsor pulled their own bill. Maybe they realized it was a bad idea, or maybe they're saving it for another day. Either way, the bill is voluntarily removed from consideration.

Related: Sponsor, Failed
Source: azleg.gov

Committees

Committee of the Whole (COW)

Committees

The entire chamber (all 60 House members or all 30 Senators) meets as one big committee. This is where floor amendments happen and bills get debated before the final vote. Think of it as the dress rehearsal before the real show.

Related: 3rd Read, Retained on Calendar
Source: azleg.gov

Standing Committee

Committees

Permanent committees that handle specific policy areas ... Education, Judiciary, Appropriations, etc. Most bills live or die in these rooms. The committee chair has enormous power over what gets heard.

Related: Assigned, Hearing
Source: azleg.gov

Interim Committee

Committees

Committees that meet between sessions to study issues. No voting power ... just research and recommendations. Think of them as study groups that report back when the legislature reconvenes.

Related: Standing Committee, Sine Die
Source: azleg.gov

Conference Committee

Committees

When the House and Senate pass different versions of the same bill, a small group from each chamber meets to work out the differences. Like couples therapy for lawmakers. The compromise they reach goes back to both chambers for a final vote.

Related: Returned to Original Chamber, Passed Amended
Source: azleg.gov

Rules Committee

Committees

Every bill must pass through Rules before it hits the floor. In the House it's called PFC (Proper for Consideration). In the Senate it's called C&P (Constitution and Proper Form). They check that the bill is constitutional and formatted correctly.

Related: C&P, PFC
Source: azleg.gov

C&P (Constitution and Proper Form)

Committees

The Senate Rules Committee review. Makes sure the bill doesn't violate the Arizona or U.S. Constitution and is properly formatted. It's a technical checkpoint, not a policy debate.

Related: Rules Committee, PFC
Source: azleg.gov

PFC (Proper for Consideration)

Committees

The House Rules Committee review. Same job as C&P but on the House side. A bill can't go to the House floor without passing PFC first.

Related: Rules Committee, C&P
Source: azleg.gov

Consent Calendar

Committees

A list of non-controversial bills that get voted on as a group. If no one objects, they all pass at once. It's the express lane. You'll see things like renaming a highway or updating a technical definition here.

Related: Consent Calendar Objected, Calendar
Source: azleg.gov

Consent Calendar Objected

Committees

A committee member objected to a bill on the consent calendar, so it gets removed and has to go through the regular process. One objection is all it takes to bump a bill out of the express lane.

Related: Consent Calendar
Source: azleg.gov

Disc/Only (Discussion Only)

Committees

A bill is discussed in committee but no vote is taken. Sometimes used to feel out support before a real hearing. Think of it as a test run ... the sponsor wants to see how the committee reacts before putting it to a vote.

Related: Hearing, Agenda
Source: azleg.gov

Agenda

Committees

The list of bills a committee plans to hear at a particular meeting. If your bill isn't on the agenda, it's not getting discussed. Committee chairs set the agenda, which gives them enormous power over what bills move forward.

Related: Hearing, Standing Committee
Source: azleg.gov

Calendar

Committees

The schedule of upcoming COW debates and floor votes. If your bill makes it here, it's getting close to a vote. The calendar is set by leadership, so being on it means the party in charge is ready to move your bill.

Related: Committee of the Whole, 3rd Read
Source: azleg.gov

Voting

Request to Speak (RTS)

Voting

An online system that lets any Arizonan register their support or opposition to a bill being heard in committee. You can even sign up to testify ... all from your phone. It's one of the most powerful civic tools in Arizona that most people don't know about.

Related: Hearing
Source: azleg.gov/requesttospeak

Caucus

Voting

A closed-door meeting of the majority or minority party to discuss strategy and bills. No votes are taken, but this is where a lot of the real decisions get made. When you hear "they worked it out in caucus," it means the deal was done behind closed doors.

Related: Majority Leaders, Minority Leaders
Source: azleg.gov

Documents

Arizona Revised Statutes (ARS)

Documents

The big book of Arizona law, organized by topic (called "titles"). When a bill is signed, it becomes part of the ARS. You'll see citations like "A.R.S. 13-1204" ... that's Title 13, Section 1204. You can look up any statute at azleg.gov.

Related: Signed, Bills Status Inquiry
Source: azleg.gov/arstitle

Bills Status Inquiry (BTS)

Documents

The online system on azleg.gov where you can track any bill's progress. Think of it as the bill's GPS tracker. You can see where it is in the process, who voted on it, what amendments were made, and when hearings are scheduled.

Related: Arizona Revised Statutes
Source: azleg.gov/bills

Initiative

Documents

A way for voters to propose new laws or constitutional amendments by gathering signatures. If enough people sign, it goes on the ballot. The legislature can't veto it ... and thanks to the Voter Protection Act, they can't easily change it after it passes, either.

Related: Referendum, Concurrent Resolution
Source: Arizona Constitution, Article IV, Part 1

Referendum

Documents

A way for voters to veto a law the legislature already passed. Gather enough signatures and the law gets put on hold until voters decide. It's the people's check on the legislature ... "we'll decide this one ourselves, thanks."

Related: Initiative, Transmit to Secretary of State
Source: Arizona Constitution, Article IV, Part 1

Concurrent Resolution

Documents

A resolution that must pass both the House and Senate. Used for things like ballot referrals, memorials to Congress, and internal rules. The governor cannot veto these ... they go directly to the Secretary of State or take effect on their own.

Related: Transmit to Secretary of State, Referendum
Source: azleg.gov

People

Speaker

People

The highest-ranking member of the House. Controls which bills get heard, assigns committee chairs, and runs floor proceedings. Arguably the most powerful person in the building. Elected by the House members at the start of each session.

Related: President, Majority Leaders
Source: Arizona Constitution, Article IV, Part 2, Section 8

President

People

The highest-ranking member of the Senate. Same power as the Speaker but on the Senate side. Sets the agenda, assigns committees, and controls the flow of legislation through the upper chamber.

Related: Speaker, Majority Leaders
Source: Arizona Constitution, Article IV, Part 2, Section 8

Majority Leaders

People

The top lieutenants of the party in power. They wrangle votes, manage the floor schedule, and keep their members in line. When a close vote is coming, these are the people working the phones.

Related: Minority Leaders, Caucus
Source: azleg.gov

Minority Leaders

People

The top members of the party NOT in power. They lead the opposition, negotiate with the majority, and try to block or amend bills they don't like. In a closely divided legislature, their leverage goes up significantly.

Related: Majority Leaders, Caucus
Source: azleg.gov

Sponsor

People

The lawmaker who introduces a bill. Their name goes on it, and they're responsible for shepherding it through the process. Bills can also have co-sponsors who support it but aren't the primary driver.

Related: Introduced, W/D (Withdrawn)
Source: azleg.gov

Calendar

Regular Session

Calendar

The 100-day session prescribed by the Arizona Constitution. Starts on the second Monday in January. In practice, sessions often run longer ... sometimes much longer. Lawmakers get a per diem for the first 120 days, which was supposed to be an incentive to wrap up on time.

Related: Sine Die, Special Session
Source: Arizona Constitution, Article IV, Part 2, Section 1

Special Session

Calendar

An emergency session called by the Governor to deal with a specific issue (like a pandemic or budget crisis). The legislature can only work on the topic the Governor specifies. No sneaking other bills in through the back door.

Related: Regular Session
Source: Arizona Constitution, Article V, Section 4

Pro Tips for Citizens

  • Use RTS! It's free, it takes 2 minutes, and lawmakers actually read these. Sign up at azleg.gov/requesttospeak.
  • Committee chairs are the most powerful gatekeepers. If they don't schedule your bill for a hearing, it's dead. Knowing who your committee chairs are matters more than most people realize.
  • Watch for strikers. A bill about "road maintenance" might suddenly become about immigration. Always check what a bill actually says, not just its title.
  • azleg.gov is your best friend. Every bill, every vote, every committee hearing is recorded there. Bookmark it. Use it. Share it.
