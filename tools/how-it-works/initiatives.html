We the People

Arizona voters don't have to wait for the legislature.

Arizona is one of the states where regular people can write their own laws. If the legislature won't act — or if you disagree with what they did — you can take it directly to the voters. Here's how, and what makes Arizona's system unique.

How It Works

The citizen initiative process in Arizona is straightforward in concept and brutal in execution. Here's the step-by-step:

  1. Write the measure. Draft the text of your proposed law or constitutional amendment. This becomes the official ballot language.
  2. File with the Secretary of State. Submit the application, including a 100-word summary and the full text.
  3. Gather signatures. This is the hard part. You need signatures from registered Arizona voters — and you have a limited time to collect them.
  4. Qualification. The Secretary of State verifies the signatures. If enough are valid, the measure qualifies for the next general election ballot.
  5. Campaign. Supporters and opponents campaign for and against the measure.
  6. Election. Voters approve or reject the measure by simple majority.
  7. It's law. If approved, the measure becomes law — and the Voter Protection Act kicks in.

There's also the referendum — where voters can challenge a law the legislature already passed. Gather enough signatures within 90 days of the session ending, and the law is suspended until voters decide. Same signature thresholds apply.

The Signature Gauntlet

Getting a measure on the ballot requires a staggering number of valid signatures from registered Arizona voters. The thresholds are tied to turnout in the most recent gubernatorial election:

  • Statutory initiative (new law): Signatures equal to 10% of votes cast for governor in the most recent gubernatorial election.
  • Constitutional amendment: Signatures equal to 15% of votes cast for governor.

Based on the 2022 gubernatorial election, the current thresholds are approximately 255,949 for a statutory initiative and 383,923 for a constitutional amendment.

The filing deadline is typically early July of the election year.

Gathering nearly 400,000 valid signatures is no joke. You need way more than the minimum because many signatures get thrown out — wrong district, illegible, not registered. Most successful campaigns gather 50–100% more signatures than required. This costs money — lots of it — which is why well-funded campaigns are more likely to make the ballot.

What about Prop 134? In 2024, the legislature tried to add a geographic distribution requirement through Prop 134, which would have required signatures from all 30 legislative districts. Voters rejected it 59.5% to 40.5%.

Source: Arizona Secretary of State, ARS §19-102

The Voter Protection Act — Your Shield

This is the most important thing on this entire page. Read it twice.

In 1998, Arizona voters passed Proposition 105, the Voter Protection Act. It's one of the strongest voter protections in the country.

Here's what it does: Once voters approve an initiative, the legislature CANNOT repeal it. Period.

They can only amend it if BOTH of these conditions are met:

  1. The amendment passes with a 3/4 supermajority in EACH chamber — that's 45 out of 60 House votes and 23 out of 30 Senate votes.
  2. The amendment must "further the purpose" of the original measure — they can't use an amendment to gut it.

The governor also cannot veto a voter-approved measure.

This is why the legislature can't just sneak changes into your ballot measures after you pass them. They've tried. Courts have blocked them.

Important Distinction

The Voter Protection Act ONLY protects citizen-initiated measures. It does NOT protect legislatively-referred ballot measures.

If the legislature refers something to the ballot and voters approve it, the legislature can change it with a simple majority the next session. Know the difference.

Source: AZ Constitution, Article IV, Part 1, Section 1, Subsection 6

Referrals vs. Initiatives: Know the Difference

These two paths to the ballot look similar on election day, but the rules behind them are wildly different.

Feature Legislative Referral Citizen Initiative
Who starts it Lawmakers Any citizen
Signatures needed None (just legislative votes) 10% (statute) or 15% (constitutional) of governor votes
Governor involvement Cannot veto Not involved
Legislative vote required Simple majority (16S / 31H) None
Voter Protection Act NOT protected Protected
Legislature can change later Yes, simple majority Only with 3/4 supermajority + must further purpose
Types Constitutional amendments, statutes Constitutional amendments, statutes
Example Prop 314 (Secure the Border) Prop 139 (Abortion Rights)

2024 General Election — 2 Citizen Initiatives

Prop 139 — Right to Abortion

PASSED • Constitutional Amendment • 61.6% Yes / 38.4% No

The "Arizona for Abortion Access" initiative establishes a fundamental right to abortion before fetal viability in the Arizona Constitution.

Backers: Arizona for Abortion Access coalition

The most expensive ballot measure campaign in Arizona history. Galvanized by the Dobbs decision and Arizona's brief revival of an 1864 near-total abortion ban.

Protected by the Voter Protection Act — the legislature cannot repeal this.

Prop 140 — Make Elections Fair Act

FAILED • Constitutional Amendment • ~41% Yes / ~59% No

Would have replaced partisan primaries with a single open primary and potentially ranked choice voting in general elections.

Backers: Make Elections Fair AZ / Chuck Coughlin, Center for the Future of Arizona

The legislature tried to preempt this with Prop 133 (constitutionalizing partisan primaries). Both failed.

2022 General Election — 2 Citizen Initiatives

Prop 209 — Predatory Debt Collection Protection Act

PASSED • Statute • ~72% Yes / ~28% No

Caps interest on medical debt and increases property and earnings exempt from debt collection.

Backers: Healthcare Rising Arizona, SEIU United Healthcare Workers

One of the most lopsided victories in recent Arizona ballot measure history.

Protected by the Voter Protection Act.

Prop 211 — Voters' Right to Know Act

PASSED • Statute • ~72% Yes / ~28% No

Requires disclosure of original funding sources for large campaign expenditures ($50K+ statewide, $25K+ local).

Backers: "Voters' Right to Know" committee / "Stop Dark Money" coalition

Survived a legal challenge by the Goldwater Institute. The court upheld it.

Protected by the Voter Protection Act.

Your Voice, Your Power

You don't need to start an initiative to make your voice heard. There are tools available to you right now:

  • Request to Speak (RTS) — Use the RTS system to weigh in on bills in the legislature. It's free and you can do it from your couch.
  • Track legislation — Follow what's moving, what's dying, and what organizations recommend at cactus.watch.
  • Find your legislators — Look up your district, your representatives, and their voting records using the District Finder at az-civic-tools.
  • Start your own initiative — The Arizona Secretary of State's office has resources for launching a citizen initiative at azsos.gov.
