Ballot Referrals

When lawmakers skip the governor and go straight to the voters

The Arizona Legislature has a powerful tool: they can put constitutional amendments and new laws directly on the ballot, bypassing the governor entirely. No signatures needed. Here's how it works... and every time they've done it recently.

How It Works

The referral process is straightforward. No signature drives, no petition deadlines, no begging voters to sign outside a grocery store. The legislature just... does it.

  1. A lawmaker introduces a concurrent resolution (SCR or HCR). Not a regular bill. A concurrent resolution is a special vehicle that doesn't go to the governor.
  2. It passes both chambers by simple majority — 16 votes in the Senate, 31 in the House. That's it. No supermajority required.
  3. It goes directly to the Secretary of State — NOT the governor. The governor never sees it. Can't sign it, can't veto it.
  4. The Secretary of State places it on the next general election ballot as a numbered proposition.
  5. Voters approve or reject it. Simple majority wins.

Key things to understand:

  • The governor cannot veto a concurrent resolution. Period. This is the whole point.
  • Constitutional amendments require voter approval regardless — the legislature can't amend the Arizona Constitution on their own, even if the governor would sign off.
  • The legislature can also refer regular statutes to the ballot. They don't have to... but sometimes they want voters to own a controversial decision.

Why Lawmakers Use This Trick

When the governor keeps vetoing your bills, you go around them. That's exactly what happened in 2024. Governor Hobbs vetoed dozens of Republican-priority bills, so the Republican-controlled legislature referred many of them directly to voters as ballot propositions.

This isn't new or necessarily bad — it's how Arizona got its Lieutenant Governor (Prop 131, 2022). But it can also be used to push controversial changes that couldn't survive a governor's veto.

Source: AZ Constitution, Article IV, Part 1

The Voter Protection Act Doesn't Protect These

Here's something most voters don't realize: the Voter Protection Act (Prop 105, 1998) does NOT protect legislatively-referred measures.

If voters approve a citizen initiative, the legislature needs a 3/4 supermajority to change it, and the changes must "further the purpose" of the original measure. That's a high bar.

But if voters approve a legislatively-referred measure? The legislature can amend or repeal it with a simple majority the very next session. No supermajority. No "furthering the purpose" requirement. Nothing.

So when you vote YES on a legislative referral, you're trusting the legislature not to change it later. With a citizen initiative, the law is locked in.

Source: AZ Constitution, Article IV, Part 1, Section 1, Subsection 6

2024 General Election — 11 Referrals

All 6 constitutional amendments failed. 3 of 5 referred statutes passed. Voters drew a clear line: mess with the Constitution and we'll shut you down; give us something sensible and we'll consider it.

Constitutional Amendments (Props 133-138) — All Failed

Prop 133 — Constitutionalize Partisan Primaries

HCR 2033 (2023) | Constitutional Amendment

Would have locked partisan primaries into the Arizona Constitution, blocking the open primary reform movement. A preemptive strike against voters who wanted more choices.

Result: Failed — 40.8% Yes / 59.2% No

Prop 134 — Geographic Signature Distribution for Initiatives

SCR 1015 (2023) | Constitutional Amendment

Would have required initiative signatures from all 30 legislative districts, making it dramatically harder to qualify ballot measures. Rural districts with tiny populations could effectively veto any initiative.

Result: Failed — 40.5% Yes / 59.5% No

Prop 135 — Auto-Terminate Emergency Declarations

HCR 2039 (2023) | Constitutional Amendment

Would have automatically ended the governor's emergency declarations after 30 days unless the legislature voted to extend. Born from COVID-era frustrations with executive power.

Result: Failed — 41.2% Yes / 58.8% No

Prop 136 — Pre-Election Challenges to Initiatives

SCR 1041 (2024) | Constitutional Amendment

Would have allowed lawsuits challenging the constitutionality of citizen initiatives before the election. Kill ballot measures in court before voters even get a chance to weigh in.

Result: Failed — 36.1% Yes / 63.9% No

Prop 137 — End Term Limits for Judges

SCR 1044 (2024) | Constitutional Amendment

Would have eliminated term limits and retention elections for Supreme Court justices and appellate judges. The biggest loss on the ballot — voters were not interested in giving judges lifetime appointments.

Result: Failed — 21.4% Yes / 78.6% No

Prop 138 — Lower Tipped Worker Minimum Wage

SCR 1040 (2024) | Constitutional Amendment

Would have allowed employers to pay tipped workers 25% less than minimum wage. Voters passed the minimum wage increase by initiative in 2016 — they weren't about to let the legislature carve it up.

Result: Failed — 23% Yes / 77% No

Referred Statutes (Props 311-315)

Prop 311 — First Responder Death Benefit

SCR 1006 (2024) | Referred Statute

$250,000 death benefit for first responders killed in the line of duty, funded by a $20 fee on criminal convictions. Hard to vote against.

Result: Passed — ~63% Yes / ~37% No

Prop 312 — Property Tax Refund for Non-Enforcement

HCR 2023 (2024) | Referred Statute

Property tax refund for costs incurred when local governments fail to enforce public nuisance laws. Framed around homelessness and encampment issues.

Result: Passed — 56.7% Yes / 43.3% No

Prop 313 — Mandatory Life for Child Sex Trafficking

SCR 1021 (2024) | Referred Statute

Mandatory life sentence for anyone convicted of child sex trafficking. The kind of proposition that passes in every state, every time.

Result: Passed — ~61.7% Yes / ~38.3% No

Prop 314 — Secure the Border Act

HCR 2060 (2024) | Referred Statute

Created a state crime for noncitizens to cross the border outside a port of entry. Gave state and local police arrest authority. Arizona's version of border enforcement politics.

Result: Passed — ~63% Yes / ~37% No

Prop 315 — Legislative Approval for Costly Rules

SCR 1012 (2024) | Referred Statute

Would have required legislative approval for any agency rules that increase regulatory costs by $500,000 or more over five years. Sounds reasonable on paper; in practice, it would have let the legislature veto environmental and health regulations.

Result: Failed — ~45% Yes / ~55% No

2022 General Election — 7 Referrals

Prop 128 — Legislature Can Repeal Initiatives

SCR 1034 (2021) | Constitutional Amendment

Would have allowed the legislature to repeal entire voter-approved initiatives if courts find any single provision unconstitutional. One bad clause and the whole thing dies. Voters saw through it.

Result: Failed — ~34% Yes / ~66% No

Prop 129 — Single-Subject Rule for Initiatives

HCR 2001 (2021) | Constitutional Amendment

Requires citizen-initiated ballot measures to address only one subject. A reasonable-sounding reform that can also be used to challenge complex initiatives in court.

Result: Passed — ~55% Yes / ~45% No

Prop 130 — Property Tax Exemptions

SCR 1011 (2022) | Constitutional Amendment

Allows the legislature to set property tax exemption amounts and qualifications. Gives lawmakers more flexibility on who gets tax breaks.

Result: Passed — ~64% Yes / ~36% No

Prop 131 — Create Lieutenant Governor

SCR 1024 (2022) | Constitutional Amendment

Creates the office of Lieutenant Governor, elected on a joint ticket with the governor. Arizona was one of the few states without one. Takes effect in 2026.

Result: Passed — ~54% Yes / ~46% No

Prop 132 — 60% Supermajority for Tax Measures

HCR 2015 (2022) | Constitutional Amendment

Requires a 60% supermajority to pass ballot measures that approve new taxes. Barely passed itself with ~51%. Directly designed to block Prop 209 (Tipped Worker Protection Act) on the same ballot.

Result: Passed — ~51% Yes / ~49% No

Prop 308 — In-State Tuition for Non-Citizen Residents

SCR 1044 (2021) | Referred Statute

Allows in-state tuition rates for non-citizen residents who graduated from Arizona high schools. A bipartisan referral — DREAM Act kids who grew up here shouldn't pay out-of-state rates.

Result: Passed — ~51% Yes / ~49% No

Prop 310 — Sales Tax for Fire Districts

HB 2901 (2022) | Referred Statute

A 0.1% sales tax increase to fund fire district operations statewide. Voters don't love tax increases, even small ones for firefighters.

Result: Failed — ~48% Yes / ~52% No

What the Data Tells Us

In 2024, voters rejected all 6 constitutional amendment referrals. That's not a coincidence — voters are skeptical of politicians trying to change the Constitution. The average "No" vote across those six amendments was over 63%.

Voters are more receptive to referred statutes, especially those with clear, popular purposes. First responder benefits, child safety, border enforcement — these passed comfortably. But even among statutes, the one that tried to give the legislature veto power over agency rules (Prop 315) failed.

Several 2024 referrals — Props 133, 134, and 136 — were specifically designed to make it harder for citizens to use their own initiative power. Voters saw through it. Every single one failed by double digits.

The pattern is clear: when the legislature tries to limit voter power, voters say no. When the legislature refers broadly popular policies, voters say yes. The ballot referral is a powerful tool, but voters aren't rubber stamps.
